    Case Study: Building a Single Source of Truth¶

    When I joined an IT managed-services company, the documentation was a mess — and I mean that in the most ordinary way, because it's a mess a lot of growing companies end up with. It was spread across four different platforms: IT Glue, SharePoint, Halo, and BookStack. My job became pulling all of that into one governed knowledge base, and I ran it as a real project rather than a cleanup. I also built out a company-wide intranet alongside it.

    The problem¶

    Documentation lived in four disconnected systems. The same procedure might exist in three of them, in three slightly different versions, with no clear owner and no way to tell which one was current. For a managed-services company, that's genuinely expensive: people can't find the right answer, the duplicates start contradicting each other, and nothing is actually governed.

    What we needed was a single source of truth — one place that's authoritative, structured, and owned.

    Running it as a project¶

    I didn't want to treat this as something I'd chip away at between other work, so I scoped it as a formal project and brought in the PMO to give it real structure and accountability. The whole thing ran close to a year, with an intense two-month stretch up front where we inventoried and assessed hundreds of documents across all four platforms.

    Honestly, treating it as a managed project is the main reason it worked at that scale. It gave the effort defined phases, stakeholders who were accountable, and a controlled way to deal with a body of content that had grown for years without anyone governing it.

    The vetting process¶

    Here's the thing about consolidation: it's not moving files, it's deciding what deserves to survive. If I'd just migrated everything, I'd have carried all the existing mess straight into the new system. So every document got vetted — and I leaned on the people who actually knew the content. I worked with subject-matter experts across the teams to look at each document and ask three questions:

    • Is it still relevant?
    • Is it outdated and in need of a refresh?
    • Does it need to change before we can trust it?

    Anything obsolete, duplicated, or superseded didn't make the cut, and that was the point. What moved into the new knowledge base was current, validated, and backed by the people who own that area.

    Building the single source of truth¶

    Once the vetted content was consolidated into Halo as the single source of truth, I applied the governance layer on top: the standard template, consistent metadata, document typing, naming conventions, and classification. I did this after the content was unified, on purpose — so the new knowledge base was consistent and governed from the day it went live, instead of inheriting four systems' worth of mismatched formatting.

    Extending it: the SharePoint intranet¶

    Alongside the knowledge base, I built a company-wide intranet in SharePoint so the whole organization had one home for shared resources. I also set up departmental sites, so each team had its own repository for files and a place to push updates and announcements out to everyone else. That turned documentation from something people only pulled from into a platform the company actually communicated through.

    How it turned out¶

    The consolidation gave us exactly what the fragmentation had been costing:

    • One authoritative place to look, instead of four that disagreed.
    • Way less duplication and far fewer contradictions, because the vetting cleared them out.
    • Faster answers — people stopped hunting across platforms.
    • Easier onboarding, since new hires learn one system instead of four.
    • Audit readiness, because everything is now governed, owned, classified, and on a lifecycle.

    What made it work¶

    If I had to point to two decisions, it's these: treating the whole thing as a governed project instead of a tidy-up, and vetting through the SMEs instead of migrating everything wholesale. Designing the structure first and only pouring validated content into it — rather than lifting four systems' disorder into a fifth — is what produced a single source of truth the company could genuinely rely on.
