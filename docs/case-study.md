# Case Study: Building a Single Source of Truth

When I joined Alliance Technologies, operational documentation was scattered across four platforms — IT Glue, SharePoint, Halo, and BookStack — with no authoritative source. I led the consolidation of that fragmented content into a single governed knowledge base, run as a formal project, and extended it with a company-wide intranet for shared and departmental resources.

## The problem

Documentation lived in four disconnected systems. The same procedure might exist in multiple places, in conflicting versions, with no clear owner and no way to know which copy was current. For a managed-services provider, that fragmentation is costly: staff can't reliably find authoritative information, duplication breeds contradiction, and nothing is governed.

The goal was a **single source of truth** — one place, authoritative, structured, and owned.

## Running it as a project

Rather than treat this as ad-hoc cleanup, I scoped it as a formal project and **engaged the PMO** to give it structure, governance, and accountability. The full effort ran for nearly a year, with a focused analysis phase of roughly two months in which we inventoried and assessed hundreds of documents across the four platforms.

Treating it as a managed project — not a side task — is what made it succeed at that scale. It brought defined phases, stakeholder accountability, and a controlled approach to a body of content that had grown without governance.

## The vetting process

Consolidation isn't relocation — it's curation. Migrating everything would have carried the existing disorder into the new system. So every document went through a vetting process driven by the people who actually knew the content: I **engaged subject-matter experts** across teams to assess each document against three questions:

- Is it still **relevant**?
- Is it **outdated** and in need of revision?
- Does it need to **change** before it can be trusted as authoritative?

Documents that were obsolete, duplicated, or superseded did not survive the cut — a deliberate outcome. What moved into the new knowledge base was validated, current, and SME-endorsed.

## Structuring the single source of truth

With vetted content consolidated into **Halo** as the single source of truth, I applied the governance layer: the standard document **template**, consistent **metadata**, document **typing**, naming conventions, and classification. Structure was applied once the content was unified — so the new knowledge base was consistent and governed from the moment it went live, rather than inheriting four systems' worth of mismatched formats.

## Extending it: the SharePoint intranet

Alongside the knowledge base, I built a **company-wide intranet in SharePoint** to give the whole organization a home for shared resources. I created **departmental sites** so each team had a repository for its files and a place to publish updates and information to the rest of the company — turning documentation from a place people *retrieved* from into a platform the organization *communicated* through.

## The outcome

The consolidation delivered exactly what fragmentation had denied:

- **One authoritative place to look** — a single source of truth instead of four competing ones.
- **Reduced duplication and conflicting information** — vetting removed the contradictions.
- **Faster information retrieval** — staff find current information without hunting across platforms.
- **Smoother onboarding** — new team members learn one system, not four.
- **Audit readiness** — governed, owned, classified content with a clear lifecycle.

## What made it work

The decisive choices were treating consolidation as a **governed project** rather than a cleanup task, and **vetting through SMEs** rather than migrating wholesale. Designing the structure and applying it to validated content — instead of lifting four systems' disorder into a fifth — is what produced a single source of truth the organization could actually trust.
