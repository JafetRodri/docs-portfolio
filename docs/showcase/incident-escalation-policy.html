Executive Summary

This policy defines how Meridian detects, classifies, and escalates security incidents — who gets called, how fast, and who has the authority to act. Because it lays out our response capability and contains the on-call escalation tree, it's held at TLP:RED and kept to the people who actually run incident response.

1. Purpose and Scope

1.1 Purpose

To make sure every security incident follows the same fast, controlled escalation path, so nothing critical sits unattended and the right people are pulled in at the right time.

1.2 Scope

Applies to all confirmed and suspected security incidents affecting Meridian-managed environments, and to every member of the Incident Response Team, Security Operations, and leadership in the escalation chain.

2. Definitions

TermDefinition
Security IncidentAny confirmed or suspected event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of a managed system.
EscalationRaising an incident to a higher authority when its severity or impact exceeds the current responder's mandate.
Containment AuthorityThe role permitted to approve disruptive containment actions, such as isolating a host or disabling an account.
On-Call TreeThe ordered list of named responders and their backups for each severity level.

3. Severity Classification

Every incident gets a severity rating at triage. The rating drives how fast it escalates and how far up the chain it goes.

SeverityDefinitionInitial Response
SEV-1 CriticalActive breach, ransomware, or outage affecting a client's core systems.Immediate, within 15 minutes
SEV-2 HighConfirmed threat with contained impact, or a credible imminent risk.Within 30 minutes
SEV-3 ModerateSuspicious activity needing investigation, no confirmed impact yet.Within 2 hours

4. Escalation Requirements

Core Rule

No SEV-1 or SEV-2 incident is ever handled by a single responder alone. Escalation is mandatory and must be acknowledged, not just sent.

4.1 Triggering Escalation

  1. Escalate the moment an incident is rated SEV-1 or SEV-2, or whenever a SEV-3 shows signs of spreading.
  2. Escalate by the on-call tree in order; if the primary doesn't acknowledge within the response window, move to the backup automatically.
  3. Every escalation is logged with the time, who was contacted, and the action taken.

4.2 Containment Authority

Disruptive containment — isolating a host, disabling an account, cutting a connection — needs sign-off from the designated Containment Authority for that severity. For SEV-1, the IR Lead can authorize containment immediately and brief leadership right after.

Do Not Wait

If an active breach is spreading and the Containment Authority can't be reached, the senior responder on shift contains first and documents the decision. Stopping the bleed comes before the paperwork.

5. Roles and Decision Authority

RoleResponsibilityAuthority

6. Escalation Workflow

1

Detect

Alert or report comes in and is logged.

2

Triage

First Responder rates severity.

3

Escalate

On-call tree engaged per severity.

4

Contain

Authorized action stops the impact.

5

Close

Resolve, notify if required, review.

7. Compliance

Escalation timing and decisions are reviewed after every SEV-1 and SEV-2. A missed escalation window or an unlogged containment decision is treated as a control failure and goes into the post-incident review with a corrective action.
