## The problem

- **Duplication** — the same procedure existed in multiple versions with no authoritative source.

- **Inconsistent structure** — every author followed their own format.

## The approach

I structured the migration in defined phases rather than a bulk lift-and-shift, because consolidation — not just relocation — was the goal.

**1. Inventory and audit.** Catalogued all existing content, identifying duplicates, owners, and currency. Many documents were stale or redundant and did not survive the migration — a deliberate outcome.

**2. Target architecture.** Designed the destination structure first: article types, naming conventions, classification scheme, and lifecycle states. Content was migrated *into a system*, not dumped into a new location.

**3. Standardization.** Reformatted content to a consistent house style and structure during migration, so the target KB was uniform from day one.

**4. Governance integration.** Assigned ownership, classification, and review dates as content landed — establishing lifecycle control as part of the migration rather than as a later cleanup.

**5. Validation.** Verified migrated content for completeness and accuracy, and confirmed cross-references resolved correctly in the new structure.

## What made it work

The decisive choice was treating this as a **consolidation and governance initiative, not a file transfer**. Designing the target architecture before moving anything meant the new knowledge base was structured, deduplicated, and governed from the moment it went live — rather than inheriting the disorder of the old system in a new location.
